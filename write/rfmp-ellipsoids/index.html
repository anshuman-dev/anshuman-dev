When geometry doesn't help (yet)

I spent a few weeks applying a geometric deep learning technique to robot arms. The result was a null finding. Here's why that's the interesting part.

A robot arm moving through space is constantly changing how well it can move in different directions. At some configurations it's free — it can push left, right, up, down with roughly equal ease. At others it's near a singularity, stuck, able to move comfortably in only one direction. This directional dexterity has a mathematical object: a small matrix called the manipulability ellipsoid. Elongated means constrained. Round means free.

These ellipsoids are interesting for a practical reason. If a robot learns good manipulability profiles on one task — say, reaching left — you'd like to transfer that knowledge to a different task without starting from scratch. Noémie Jaquier's lab at KTH studies exactly this problem, and published a framework for it in 2021.

Separately, there's a recent technique called Riemannian Flow Matching — a way to learn generative models on curved mathematical spaces rather than flat Euclidean ones. Jaquier's lab also published a version of this for robot motion in 2024.

The question I set out to answer: can you combine the two? Apply Riemannian Flow Matching directly to the space of manipulability ellipsoids and generate new ones conditioned on a task descriptor? Nobody had done it. I built the pipeline to find out.

The geometry problem

Manipulability ellipsoids are symmetric positive-definite (SPD) matrices. That sounds technical but the key point is simple: you can't treat them like regular numbers. The space they live in is curved — like the surface of a sphere, not a flat plane. If you try to average two ellipsoids by just adding their entries and dividing by two, you can get nonsense. The right way to interpolate between two points on a curved space is along a geodesic — the curved-space equivalent of a straight line.

The naive shortcut is to pick one point on the manifold, flatten everything to a tangent plane at that point, do your math in flat space, and map back. This is called the single tangent space approximation. Jaquier's lab published a paper in 2024 specifically titled "Unraveling the Single Tangent Space Fallacy" — showing this breaks down when your data is spread widely across the curved space. I wanted to test whether that fallacy actually shows up in practice for manipulability data.

What I built

Everything runs on a MacBook — no GPU server, no simulator, no robot. The SPD matrices for a robot arm are small (3×3 or 6×6), the networks are small, and robot kinematics is just math. I generated a synthetic dataset by simulating a 3-joint planar arm moving through 500 random trajectories, computing a manipulability ellipsoid at each step.

Then I trained three models on the same data:

RFM (mine): Riemannian Flow Matching on the SPD manifold. Geodesics, curved interpolation, the full geometric treatment. The neural network learns a vector field on the manifold and generates new ellipsoids by integrating it.

Euclidean baseline: Flatten the ellipsoid to a 3-number vector, run standard flow matching in flat space, reconstruct. Geometry completely ignored.

Single tangent space baseline: The "wrong but common" approach — flatten everything to one tangent plane, train, map back. On the manifold technically, but only with a flat approximation.

What happened

Before I even looked at the results, something interesting emerged during training. The Euclidean and single tangent space models refused to train stably with the standard flow matching objective. The training loss would swing by factors of a thousand between epochs. The Riemannian model trained smoothly from the start.

The reason is geometric. Standard flow matching targets a velocity (endpoint − noise) / (1 − t) — as t approaches 1, this blows up. The Riemannian version doesn't have this problem: the log map on the manifold naturally goes to zero as you approach the target, keeping the velocity bounded throughout. I had to switch to a different parameterisation for the flat baselines to stabilise them.

The actual performance numbers:

Method Mean distance ↓ Std
Euclidean FM 4.25 2.51
RFM (mine) 3.66 2.19
Single Tangent Space FM 3.13 0.91

The single tangent space method won on mean distance. The method the 2024 paper says is a fallacy performed best on my dataset. RFM beat the Euclidean baseline but didn't beat the supposedly wrong approximation.

Why this is the interesting part

The single tangent space fallacy is real — but it's conditional. It shows up when your data is spread far across the manifold, far enough that the curvature matters. My synthetic dataset, a 3-joint planar arm with randomly sampled joint angles, doesn't stress the geometry enough. The ellipsoids live in a moderate region of the SPD manifold. The curvature is there but not dramatic enough for the approximation to visibly fail.

A null finding with a clear explanation of why it's null is more useful than a positive result you don't understand.

What would make the fallacy appear? Near-singular configurations where ellipsoids become nearly degenerate — very flat, close to zero eigenvalues, lying in extreme corners of the manifold. Or transferring between robots with very different kinematics, which would force the data to span a much wider region. Or moving from 2D position to 6D pose, where the 6×6 SPD manifold has much higher intrinsic curvature.

These are testable hypotheses. The pipeline exists. The comparison infrastructure is in place. Running it on a harder dataset is a concrete next step — not a vague "future work" gesture but an actual experiment I know how to run.

What I came away with

I went in expecting to confirm that geometry helps. I came out with evidence that it sometimes helps, under conditions I can now characterise. That's more useful.

The training stability result is real and not dataset-dependent. Riemannian flow matching is inherently better conditioned than flat flow matching because the manifold geometry provides natural bounds on the velocity field. That advantage exists regardless of whether the curvature is large enough to affect final performance.

The full code is on GitHub, including a test suite that verifies all the manifold operations (exp map, log map, geodesic) before any training runs. The technical report is in the repo. Everything runs on a Mac.

This project was built as a portfolio piece for a PhD application to Noémie Jaquier's Geometric Robot Learning Lab at KTH. Whether or not that goes anywhere, I learned more about Riemannian geometry in three weeks than in the previous three years of reading about it abstractly. Building things has a way of doing that.
